    About GRAVIS

    Galactic Rotation from Field Dynamics

    Gravity Field Dynamics (GFD) is the operational gravitational framework derived from the covariant completion of Dual Tetrad Gravity, enabling zero-parameter predictions of galactic rotation and related phenomena. GRAVIS is the modeling engine that implements it.

    The progression from theory to tool follows four steps. The geometric origin is Dual Tetrad Topology: two tetrahedral field chambers coupled at a shared center, the Field Origin. The covariant completion produces a zero-parameter scalar-tensor action in which the Lagrangian, the field equation, and the acceleration scale are all derived from the topology. The working formulation is Gravity Field Dynamics, the field-level theory used for prediction, inference, and comparison. The software implementation is GRAVIS, the modeling engine that computes rotation curves, infers mass, and validates against observations.

    The Problem

    Stars in the outer regions of spiral galaxies orbit faster than Newtonian gravity predicts from visible matter alone. This discrepancy appears consistently across hundreds of galaxies spanning four orders of magnitude in mass (Lelli et al. 2016). The standard resolution posits dark matter halos containing approximately 85% of each galaxy's gravitating mass, yet four decades of direct-detection experiments have produced no confirmed particle.

    An alternative possibility: the discrepancy reflects not missing mass but missing structure in the gravitational field itself. If the field possesses internal coupling architecture that modifies its response below a characteristic acceleration scale, the observed rotation curves may follow from baryonic matter alone.

    Dual Tetrad Gravity

    The tetrad formulation of general relativity decomposes the metric into four basis vectors at each spacetime point, forming a tetrahedron: the minimal gravitational field chamber in three spatial dimensions. Four independent arguments from stability analysis, spinor structure, chirality, and the bimetric gravity framework establish that two such field chambers are required, coupled with opposite orientations to form a stellated octahedron sharing a common center: the Field Origin.

    The coupling hierarchy of this structure is governed by a polynomial in the simplex number k = 4:

    f(k) = 1 + k + k2

    The three terms encode the three structural levels: the Field Origin (1), the face coupling channels (k = 4), and the full field interaction between both chambers (k2 = 16, all four faces of one coupling to all four faces of the other). This polynomial, combined with the topology, uniquely determines the Lagrangian, the acceleration scale, and the complete gravitational action. There are no free parameters.

    Gravity Field Dynamics

    Gravity Field Dynamics is the operational formulation that emerges from the covariant completion. It provides a closed-form field equation relating the true gravitational field to the Newtonian field at every radius. Given the enclosed baryonic mass, GFD computes the predicted rotation velocity in a single algebraic step: no numerical integration, no iteration, no fitting.

    The topology determines a characteristic acceleration scale below which the field cannot sustain full coupling across all k2 = 16 channels:

    a0 = k2 G me / re2 ≈ 1.22 × 10−10 m/s2

    Above this scale, gravity is Newtonian. Below it, the field enters partial coupling and the effective gravitational response is enhanced. This is why rotation curves are flat at large radii: the acceleration drops below a0, the field enters partial coupling, and the velocity remains higher than Newton predicts.

    The field equation is bidirectional. The forward direction takes mass and predicts velocity. The inverse direction takes velocity and infers mass. Both are algebraic. This bidirectionality is what enables GRAVIS to operate in both prediction mode and inference mode from the same Lagrangian.

    Relationship to MOND and CDM

    In 1983, Milgrom proposed an empirical modification to Newtonian dynamics that successfully predicts rotation curves across the full galaxy population from baryonic mass alone (McGaugh 2011; Lelli et al. 2017). What MOND lacks is a derivation: both the interpolating function and the acceleration scale are empirical inputs.

    GFD derives both from the topology of the gravitational field. What Milgrom discovered empirically, the field dynamics produces from the Lagrangian. This is why the GFD and MOND curves appear similar on every rotation curve plot: they share the same deep-field limit. They differ in the transition regime, and crucially, in origin.

    The standard cosmological model achieves the same empirical fit by adding a dark matter halo with at least 2 free parameters per galaxy. GRAVIS displays the CDM prediction alongside GFD so the reader can see both matching the observations: one with zero parameters, the other with two or more.

    What GRAVIS Computes

    For each galaxy, GRAVIS constructs the enclosed baryonic mass from three independently measured components: a Hernquist stellar bulge, an exponential stellar disk, and an exponential gas disk. Stellar masses derive from Spitzer 3.6 μm photometry. Gas masses derive from 21 cm HI observations. No component is fitted to rotation curve data.

    GRAVIS evaluates four rotation curves on the same plot:

    • Newtonian: the baseline prediction from visible matter alone.
    • GFD (Dual Tetrad): the zero-parameter prediction from the covariant completion.
    • Classical MOND: the empirical Milgrom formulation with the same acceleration scale.
    • CDM + NFW: the standard dark matter prediction, fitted to observations when available.

    The multi-point consistency panel in inference mode goes further: it tests whether every observation point independently infers the same total baryonic mass. Agreement across the full radial range, without parameter tuning, is the non-trivial result. See the FAQ for the full mathematical detail.

    Field Dynamics

    The coupling between the two field chambers produces three structural levels, encoded in the polynomial f(k) = 1 + k + k2, where k = 4 is the simplex number. The first term is the Field Origin itself: the shared center where both chambers interact. The second term counts the four face-to-face coupling channels. The third term counts all sixteen cross-chamber couplings, every face of one chamber to every face of the other.

    Below a characteristic acceleration scale, the field can no longer sustain full coupling across all k2 = 16 channels. It enters partial coupling, and the effective gravitational response is enhanced. This is field dynamics: the internal structure of the gravitational field governing its own strength.

Exploring the Gravity Field

A guided introduction to the internal structure of gravity

Gravity Is a Field

Gravity is a field. Not a force that reaches across empty space. Not a mysterious pull between distant objects. It lattices spacetime, carries its own topology, and propagates. In September 2015, the LIGO observatory recorded this directly: a ripple in the gravity field, produced by two merging black holes over a billion light years away, arrived at Earth as a gravitational wave. Two years later, a neutron star collision confirmed that these waves travel at exactly the speed of light.

This is the starting point. Gravity is a field, and disturbances in that field travel outward as waves, at the speed of light, through the structure of spacetime itself. But a field that carries waves cannot be a single undifferentiated whole. An ocean is not one enormous water molecule. It is a topology of water molecules, and it is precisely that topology, molecule coupled to molecule in a connected lattice, that allows a wave to propagate across it. The question that follows is the one this page is built to answer: if the gravity field carries waves, what is the topology of that field?

Field Propagation and Field Neighbours

Consider how an ocean wave moves across open water. No single water molecule travels from shore to shore. Instead, each molecule pushes its immediate neighbours, and those neighbours push theirs, and the disturbance passes forward through a chain of local interactions. The wave exists because the water has structure: molecules arranged in proximity, each one coupled to the ones around it.

Gravitational waves work the same way. Each region of the gravity field influences its immediate neighbours through the geometric structure that general relativity requires. The disturbance passes from region to region, at the speed of light, across billions of light years. This is not metaphor. The wave equation that describes gravitational wave propagation contains a term called the Laplacian, and the Laplacian, by mathematical definition, computes the difference between a point and its surrounding neighbours. No neighbours, no Laplacian. No Laplacian, no wave equation. No wave equation, no gravitational waves.

LIGO detected gravitational waves. Therefore gravity has neighbours. Therefore the gravity field is not a single, monolithic entity. It is a topology: many locally coupled field regions, each one interacting with the ones adjacent to it, forming a connected structure that spans all of spacetime.

The gravity field has parts. And those parts have geometry.

Field Organisation: The k = 4 Topology

If the gravity field is a topology of locally coupled regions, the next question is immediate: how are those regions organised? How many neighbours does each field region couple to? Is the arrangement random, or does it follow a rule?

It follows a rule. The number of field coupling faces per region is exactly four. This value, written as k = 4, is not chosen by convention or fitted to data. It is squeezed from both directions by independent constraints, one from pure geometry and one from direct observation.

From geometry: in three spatial dimensions, the simplest possible shape that encloses a volume is a tetrahedron, a solid with exactly four triangular faces. This is the three-dimensional equivalent of a triangle being the simplest shape that encloses area in two dimensions. You cannot enclose a volume with fewer than four faces. Geometry itself sets the floor: k must be at least 4.

From observation: LIGO measured exactly two polarisations of gravitational waves. Where do those two come from? When TetA and TetB couple, the field propagates through k = 4 shared faces. Each face is a triangle, a two-dimensional surface, and a wave passing through that face can oscillate in exactly two independent directions within its plane. Those two transverse directions are the two polarisations: plus and cross. If the field had fewer faces, more independent oscillation modes would remain unconstrained. If it had more, some faces would be redundant and gravity could not propagate at all. Only k = 4 produces exactly two polarisations, matching what LIGO observed. Observation sets the ceiling: k cannot exceed 4.

The floor is 4. The ceiling is 4. The gravity field is k = 4 organised.

The simplex theorem states that in d spatial dimensions, the minimum convex polytope that encloses volume has exactly d + 1 faces. For d = 3: the tetrahedron, with 4 faces. This is not a choice. Three dimensions force it.

The LIGO constraint works from the other direction. When TetA and TetB couple, the gravitational field propagates through their shared faces. Each face is a triangle, a two-dimensional surface. Each field region, then, couples to exactly four neighbours through four shared boundary faces. Four faces, arranged symmetrically around a center, form a tetrahedron. This tetrahedron is not an abstract mathematical convenience. It is the shape of a single gravity field region: the minimal enclosed volume in three dimensions. In the language of field dynamics, it is called a field chamber.

Every field region has a characteristic size. The electromagnetic field has its photon wavelength. The strong nuclear field operates within the radius of a proton. The gravity field is no different. When two independent domains of physics converge on the same scale, the scale is not a coincidence. It is telling you that the same underlying structure governs both. The gravity field tile and the electromagnetic field share a common characteristic length, because they are two aspects of the same topology.

Two Field Chambers, Not One

A single tetrahedral field chamber is not self-consistent. It has a preferred direction: the apex of the tetrahedron points somewhere, and that somewhere breaks the symmetry. A gravity field with a built-in preferred direction would produce instabilities, specifically a ghost mode, a fluctuation with negative kinetic energy that would cause the field to radiate energy without limit.

The resolution comes from a second field chamber: a second tetrahedron, oriented in the opposite direction, sharing the same center. Where the first tetrahedron points up, the second points down. Where the first has an apex, the second has a base. The two orientations cancel the asymmetry, and the ghost mode vanishes.

This requirement does not come from one argument alone. Four independent lines of established physics, developed by different research groups in different decades for different purposes, all arrive at the same conclusion: the gravity field requires two tetrahedral field chambers with opposite orientations.

4. Bimetric gravity (Hassan, Rosen 2012): Ghost-free massive gravity requires two dynamical metric fields, each with its own tetrad. The two fields couple through an interaction potential at their shared center.

When four independent roads converge on the same destination, the destination is not hypothesised. It is established.

Full Coupling and Partial Coupling

When the gravitational field is strong, all 21 coupling channels are engaged. The field is in full coupling: both field chambers are fully active, all 16 coupled sub-regions are saturated, all 4 face channels are propagating, and the Field Origin is mediating the complete interaction. In this regime, gravity behaves exactly as Newton described: the force falls off as the square of the distance.

But there is a threshold. Below a characteristic acceleration scale called a0, approximately 1.2 × 10−10 m/s2, the field can no longer sustain full coupling across all channels. The coupled sub-regions begin to disengage. The face channels lose saturation. The field enters partial coupling.

In partial coupling, the only channel that persists unconditionally is the Field Origin: the k0 = 1 term, the irreducible floor. Even when every other coupling channel has relaxed, the Field Origin remains. The gravity field never fully switches off. It simply operates through fewer channels, and the effective gravitational response changes character.

The result is that gravity transitions from falling as 1/r2 to falling as 1/r. This is precisely what astronomers observe in the outskirts of spiral galaxies, where stars orbit at velocities that remain constant with increasing distance from the galactic center. The rotation curves are flat, and they are flat because the gravity field has entered partial coupling. The internal structure of the field, not invisible matter, is responsible.
